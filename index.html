Thinking About Data

A summer primer for API-201

This fall in API-201, the real work isn’t running an analysis — it’s thinking clearly about one. Every problem set, lecture, memo, and paper rests on a chain of decisions someone made about a dataset: what went in, what happened to it, what the output actually shows, and which judgment calls quietly shaped the answer. The people who add value in a policy room are the ones who can follow that chain and say where it holds up and where it doesn’t.

That habit of mind is what this primer builds. The analyses you’ll meet happen to be written in R — the language you’ll see all fall — so you’ll learn to read it fluently enough to follow the reasoning. But the goal was never the language. The goal is to look at an analysis and say, in plain English, what it did and whether you’d trust it.

You will not write code from scratch here, and you don’t need any programming background — just curiosity and roughly ten hours between now and your arrival on campus, in whatever chunks fit your summer.

How this site works

Everything runs right in your browser — there is nothing to install, no account to create, and nothing you can break. The code on these pages is live: click Run Code under any chunk to execute it, edit it, and run it again (that’s encouraged — every chunk has a Start Over button).

NoteGive each page a moment to wake up

The first time you run code on a page, your browser spends about 20–30 seconds loading R behind the scenes (Units 2 and 4 also load a plotting library). That’s normal — after the first run, everything is instant. A laptop works much better than a phone for this.

Along the way you’ll meet three kinds of questions:

  • Multiple choice — answered right on the page, with instant feedback and a chance to retry.
  • “Explain it in plain English” — short written answers, the core skill of the course. Click Get feedback for an AI reading of your answer against the instructor’s rubric (it gives a verdict and a pointer, never the answer itself), or Show model answer to compare for yourself.
  • Predict-then-run — the habit that makes all of this stick: read the code, commit to a guess about the output, then run it.

None of it is graded or tracked. Your answers live only in your own browser, and redoing a page is a feature, not a confession.

The five units

Work through them in order — each one builds on the last.

Unit 1 — The Building Blocks · ~2 hours

The eight tidyverse “verbs” that make up almost every R script you’ll see this fall: the pipe |> plus filter, select, mutate, group_by, summarize, arrange, and left_join. One verb at a time, each with live examples on a small state-level health insurance dataset, ending with a quick quiz to check that the verbs stuck.

Unit 2 — Read a Pipeline · ~3 hours

The verbs start chaining together. Six pipelines on school-district data, growing from three lines to a scatterplot and a full regression. For each one you’ll predict the output, describe it in plain English, and then run it to check yourself. Hints early on; training wheels off by the end.

Unit 3 — Spot the Problem · ~3 hours

Everything in Units 1 and 2 was correct code. Here, nothing is. Four analyses that run without errors and produce plausible-looking output — each hiding a mistake that makes the analyst’s claim wrong, sometimes backwards. Your job: describe what the code did, find the flaw, and explain why the output misleads. The errors are catchable with careful reading and common sense about the policy world — exactly the skill that makes you valuable in a room.

Unit 4 — Full Narration · ~2 hours

The capstone. One complete 35-line analysis script — cleaning, transforming, summarizing, plotting, regression — of the kind a junior analyst might email around. You’ll narrate the whole thing for a colleague who hasn’t seen the code, and flag the one decision you’d push back on before the results get circulated.

Unit 5 — The AI Analyst · ~45 minutes

A choose-your-own-adventure finale. You have a 3 PM deadline, an AI assistant that works fast and decides things silently, and one slide to get right. Eight endings — most of them instructively embarrassing — each turning on whether you catch a mistake you learned to spot in Units 1–4. Replaying to find the other endings is the point.

When you get stuck

You’ll notice a 💬 button in the corner of every page. That’s the primer’s helper — an AI assistant that knows these pages, the datasets, and the exercises. There’s also a small “Help me understand this” link under every code box that sends that snippet straight to it.

Use it — that’s what it’s for — but a word of advice first: being stuck is where the learning happens. Reread the code, run it, change something and run it again, take a real guess. Reach for the helper when you’re genuinely stuck, not at the first flicker of confusion. It’s built to match that spirit: it starts with hints and guiding questions rather than answers, and only walks you through a solution after you’ve shown it your real attempts. (If you ask it for the answer outright, it will politely decline — don’t take it personally.)

Two practical notes: the conversation follows you from page to page, and the Copy button in its header puts the whole transcript on your clipboard if you want to save it or bring questions to us in the fall. And the helper can make mistakes — if it ever seems confused, trust the page over the chat.

Odds and ends

  • Predict before you run. It’s the single highest-value habit on this site. A guess that turns out wrong teaches more than a run you didn’t think about.
  • If a page misbehaves — a chunk hangs, output looks stale — just reload the page. Nothing is lost but the code edits in that chunk.
  • Browsers: any recent Chrome, Safari, Firefox, or Edge is fine.
  • About AI: in the real world, AI tools will happily write the R for you, and that’s fine. What they can’t do is sit in a meeting and judge whether an analysis actually answers the question — whether the data, the transformations, and the conclusion hang together. That judgment about data is what you’re building here, so let the helper guide you, and keep the thinking to yourself.
